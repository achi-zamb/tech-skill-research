Why bolt-on technical training stalls

In a smart factory, a rollout fails less often on the technology than on the people asked to trust it. The peer-reviewed and field evidence converges on one point: the soft skills aren't a "nice to have" wrapped around the real training — they are the mechanism by which technical training converts into adoption.

Resistance

Employee resistance to new technology is common in manufacturing. Where managers meet it with empathy and clear communication, productivity recovers — not because the system changed, but because people felt supported and understood.

Source · Purdue Global (faculty interviews, 2026)
Acceptance

Across 598 white- and blue-collar workers, resilience and access to information and training were positively associated with technology acceptance, which in turn correlated with higher work engagement. (Cross-sectional, self-report — association, not proven cause.)

Source · Molino, Cortese & Ghislieri, 2020 · IJERPH (MDPI)
Fear

Around a third of workers in one survey believed AI could eliminate their job. Upskilling that reframes the narrative — augmentation, not replacement — is how organizations defuse the anxiety that quietly sinks adoption.

Source · BCG, via Manufacturing Dive (2024)
Portfolio

Analysis of the Italian manufacturing sector argues technical, social, and strategic capabilities must be strengthened simultaneously — a single-axis "teach the machine" program leaves the multifaceted expertise that Industry 4.0 needs incomplete.

Source · Peer-reviewed, R&D Management (Wiley, 2026)
